Reflecting on Hypertext, Interaction, and Website Design

Revisiting Hypertext: Moulthrop’s Vision and the Modern Internet

In his 2003 piece You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media (1991), Stuart Moulthrop explores how hypertext promised to revolutionize digital communication by allowing for nonlinear, multi-path storytelling and information sharing. Reflecting on his ideas over two decades later, it’s fascinating to see how hypertext has become an integral part of the internet. Yet, rather than liberating readers from linear narratives, many modern websites and platforms have constrained interaction within algorithm-driven feeds and rigid structures.

The hypertextual vision of endless exploration still thrives on niche websites, interactive fiction, and immersive digital experiences. However, mainstream platforms favor content curation that minimizes the agency of users in navigating content. This contrast makes me wonder whether the potential of hypertext has been fully realized or compromised in pursuit of monetization and control. My project aims to embrace interactivity by giving users more control over their experience, much like Moulthrop envisioned.

Interaction and the World Wide Web: My Perspective

To me, interaction means fostering a sense of agency and engagement. I want my website to encourage exploration and discovery, reflecting how hypertext was originally imagined. Interaction isn’t just about clicking buttons or following links; it’s about creating a conversation between the user and the content. Whether it’s through intuitive navigation, interactive visuals, or dynamic content updates, I want visitors to feel like they’re actively participating in their journey through the site.

Design Decisions: Folder Structure and URL Scheme

To ensure an organized and efficient development process, I structured my website folders as follows:

project-root/ ├── index.html # Homepage ├── blog.html # Blog section ├── pages/ # Individual blog posts ├── css/ # Stylesheets ├── images/ # Visual assets and wireframes └── scripts/ # JavaScript files

This structure helps me keep related content together while maintaining clear separation between presentation (CSS), content (HTML), and behavior (JS). The posts/ folder holds individual blog entries, allowing each to have a clean, dedicated URL like: https://myportfolio.com/posts/week1.html
